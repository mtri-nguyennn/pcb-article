1 — Foundation

1. PCB anatomy and the complete design workflow

Core knowledge

  • Know the physical elements: substrate, copper, solder mask, silkscreen, pads, traces, vias and holes.
  • Distinguish a bare PCB from a populated PCBA.
  • Use a controlled workflow: requirements → schematic/ERC → placement/routing → DRC/review → manufacturing → assembly/test.
  • Treat the schematic as the logical design and the layout as its physical implementation.

Practical milestone: Explain a simple two-layer board and the files produced at each design stage.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

What is the difference between a PCB and a PCBA?

Answer: A PCB is the unpopulated printed circuit board. A PCBA is the PCB after components have been assembled onto it.
2

Which feature electrically connects copper on different layers?

Answer: A plated via or plated through-hole.
3

Put these in the correct order: routing, schematic, fabrication outputs, placement, ERC.

Answer: Schematic → ERC → placement → routing → DRC/review → fabrication outputs.
4

Which KiCad layer normally defines the physical board perimeter?

Answer: Edge.Cuts.
5

Why can a circuit with a correct schematic fail after fabrication?

Answer: Layout introduces real resistance, inductance, capacitance, thermal, return-path, mechanical and manufacturing effects that the ideal schematic does not show.

2 — Beginner

2. EDA projects and schematic capture

Core knowledge

  • Create one project containing the schematic, PCB, libraries and settings; do not manage the board as an isolated file.
  • Use reference designators, values, net labels, junctions, power symbols and readable functional blocks.
  • Annotate components and assign clear net names before layout.
  • Run ERC, investigate every warning and document intentional exceptions.

Practical milestone: Draw and validate an LED, button or sensor interface schematic in KiCad.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

What does reference designator R3 mean?

Answer: It identifies the third resistor reference in the project; it is not the resistor value.
2

Two wires cross without a junction dot. Are they necessarily connected?

Answer: No. Connection depends on the EDA tool’s junction rules; an explicit junction removes ambiguity.
3

Why use a net label such as I2C_SDA?

Answer: It names and connects a signal clearly across the schematic without drawing long wires.
4

What should you do after changing the schematic once PCB layout has started?

Answer: Run ERC again, then update/synchronize the PCB from the schematic and review the proposed changes.
5

ERC reports that a power input is not driven, although an external connector supplies it. What is the correct response?

Answer: Model the power source correctly, for example with the tool’s appropriate power-output or power-flag mechanism; do not blindly ignore the warning.

3 — Beginner

3. Datasheets, symbols, footprints and the BOM

Core knowledge

  • A symbol represents electrical function; a footprint represents the physical land pattern.
  • Verify package name, pin numbering, polarity, exposed pads, pitch and dimensions against the manufacturer datasheet.
  • Check courtyard, assembly outline and 3D orientation, but never trust the 3D model as proof of pin correctness.
  • Use exact manufacturer part numbers in the BOM when substitution matters.

Practical milestone: Create or verify one custom footprint from a mechanical drawing and print it at 1:1 scale.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

Can a generic op-amp symbol be assigned to any 8-pin footprint?

Answer: No. The package and, critically, the symbol pin numbers must match the exact device datasheet.
2

An IC is available in SOIC-8 and DFN-8. Can both use the same footprint?

Answer: No. Their pad geometry, pitch, body and often exposed-pad requirements differ.
3

What is the safest source for a footprint’s dimensions?

Answer: The latest manufacturer datasheet or official package drawing, checked against the chosen orderable part.
4

Why include an MPN in the BOM?

Answer: It identifies the exact purchasable component and prevents ambiguous substitutions based only on value or description.
5

What simple check catches many footprint-size errors before ordering?

Answer: Print the PCB or footprint at true 1:1 scale and place the real component over it; still verify pin mapping separately.

4 — Beginner to intermediate

4. Power circuits, decoupling and schematic reliability

Core knowledge

  • Design the complete power path: connector, protection, regulation, bulk capacitance and local bypassing.
  • Place local decoupling capacitors at the relevant IC power pins with a short current loop.
  • Follow datasheets for regulator input/output capacitor values, ESR, layout and stability requirements.
  • Check reset, boot straps, pull-ups, unused pins, programming headers and connector pin protection.

Practical milestone: Design a stable 5 V-to-3.3 V supply section and justify each protection and capacitor component.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

Why is a 100 nF capacitor placed close to an IC power pin?

Answer: It supplies high-frequency transient current locally and reduces the inductance of the current path.
2

Can one large capacitor at the board connector replace all local bypass capacitors?

Answer: No. Its distance and interconnect inductance make it ineffective for many fast local current transients.
3

Should regulator capacitor values be copied from an unrelated design?

Answer: No. Use the selected regulator’s datasheet and account for capacitance tolerance, bias derating and ESR requirements.
4

A pushbutton input floats when released. What is missing?

Answer: A defined pull-up or pull-down, whether external or a deliberately enabled internal pull resistor.
5

Why provide a programming/debug header on an early prototype?

Answer: It makes firmware loading, reset control and low-level debugging practical during bring-up.

5 — Intermediate

5. Board outline, layer stack-up and design rules

Core knowledge

  • Lock board dimensions, mounting holes, connector locations, height limits and keepouts before free placement.
  • Choose layer count and stack-up from electrical, routing, cost and manufacturer constraints.
  • Set clearances, minimum widths, via sizes and net classes from the fabricator and applicable safety/design requirements.
  • Use a continuous reference plane where signal return paths require it.

Practical milestone: Create a manufacturable board outline and rule set using a real fabricator’s published capabilities.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

Why should connectors and mounting holes be placed early?

Answer: They are constrained by the enclosure and external interfaces; late movement can invalidate the entire layout.
2

Is the smallest advertised track width always a good default?

Answer: No. It reduces manufacturing margin and may increase cost or risk. Use comfortably manufacturable dimensions unless density requires otherwise.
3

What determines controlled impedance?

Answer: Trace geometry, copper thickness, dielectric thickness and dielectric properties, plus the manufacturer’s actual stack-up.
4

Why is a self-intersecting board outline invalid?

Answer: The fabricator cannot unambiguously determine the board perimeter.
5

When should a four-layer board be considered instead of two layers?

Answer: When routing density, continuous reference planes, power integrity, EMI control or signal integrity cannot be met reliably on two layers.

6 — Intermediate

6. Component placement

Core knowledge

  • Place fixed mechanical parts first, then major ICs and functional blocks.
  • Place components according to current flow, signal flow and critical loops—not visual symmetry.
  • Keep decoupling, crystals, feedback components and high-current switching parts close to their associated pins.
  • Consider assembly access, orientation, thermal flow, probing and rework.

Practical milestone: Place a small MCU board so that all critical nets can be routed without crossing noisy and sensitive regions.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

Which should be placed first: a board-edge USB connector or a status LED?

Answer: The USB connector, because its mechanical position is fixed by the enclosure and cable access.
2

Why place a crystal close to MCU oscillator pins?

Answer: To minimize loop area, parasitic coupling and susceptibility to noise.
3

Is aligning every component in a neat grid the primary placement goal?

Answer: No. Electrical performance, manufacturability, thermals and mechanical constraints take priority.
4

Where should a buck regulator input capacitor be placed?

Answer: As close as the datasheet recommends to the regulator’s input and return pins, minimizing the high di/dt loop.
5

Why leave probe access around important rails and signals?

Answer: Early prototypes require measurement and debugging; inaccessible nodes slow or prevent diagnosis.

7 — Intermediate

7. Routing fundamentals, vias and copper zones

Core knowledge

  • Route critical clocks, analog nodes, power loops and constrained interfaces before ordinary GPIO.
  • Use short, direct routes and avoid unnecessary vias, stubs and neck-downs.
  • Maintain a nearby, continuous return path; add an appropriate return via when changing reference layers.
  • Use copper zones deliberately and re-run zone fill and DRC after edits.

Practical milestone: Route a two-layer board with zero unintended unrouted nets and a defensible ground strategy.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

What does an unrouted ratline indicate?

Answer: A required electrical connection from the netlist has not yet been completed with copper.
2

Why can a signal via require a nearby ground via?

Answer: When the signal changes reference layers, the return current needs a low-inductance path to change planes as well.
3

Does a filled ground zone automatically create a good ground plane?

Answer: No. Slots, narrow necks, isolated islands and poor via connections can break or increase return-path impedance.
4

Why use thermal relief on many through-hole or SMD pads connected to large copper?

Answer: It reduces heat sinking during soldering while maintaining an electrical connection.
5

Should you route a fast signal across a split in its reference plane?

Answer: No. The return current must detour, increasing loop area, noise and radiation.

8 — Intermediate to advanced

8. Power distribution, grounding, current and thermal design

Core knowledge

  • Design the complete forward-and-return current path; shared impedance is often more important than the label GND.
  • Use sufficiently short and wide copper for current, voltage drop and temperature rise; verify with IPC methods, simulation or manufacturer tools.
  • A solid ground plane is usually safer than casually splitting analog and digital ground.
  • Plan heat spreading, copper area, exposed pads, thermal vias and airflow from component loss data.

Practical milestone: Estimate voltage drop and temperature risk for a pump, LED or regulator power path and verify the layout.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

Which factors affect trace current capability?

Answer: Copper thickness, trace width and length, allowed temperature rise, surrounding copper/layers, airflow and board material conditions.
2

Why can a narrow ground return cause sensor error?

Answer: Load current creates voltage across the shared return impedance, shifting the sensor’s local ground reference.
3

Should analog and digital ground always be split?

Answer: No. Splits can force return-current detours. Partition circuits and control current paths; split only with a justified system-level plan.
4

What is Kelvin sensing?

Answer: Using separate sense connections so measurement current does not share the voltage drop of the high-current path.
5

Can thermal vias be added arbitrarily under every exposed pad?

Answer: No. Their diameter, finish, count and paste interaction must follow package, fabricator and assembler guidance.

9 — Advanced

9. Analog, mixed-signal interfaces and protection

Core knowledge

  • Partition sensitive analog, high-speed digital and noisy power sections according to current paths and coupling risk.
  • Keep ADC inputs, references, feedback and high-impedance nodes compact and away from switching nodes.
  • Place ESD protection and input filtering at the connector or noise-entry point.
  • Copy proven reference layouts for crystals, RF networks, precision converters and switching regulators unless you can justify changes.

Practical milestone: Lay out a sensor-to-ADC chain and an external connector with suitable filtering and protection.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

Where should a TVS diode for an external connector be placed?

Answer: Close to the connector, before the protected signal travels through the board, with a short return path.
2

Why keep an ADC reference bypass capacitor close to the reference pins?

Answer: The reference is sensitive to dynamic current and noise; a compact loop reduces impedance and contamination.
3

What is wrong with routing a motor phase trace beside a high-impedance sensor input?

Answer: Fast, high-current switching can couple capacitively and inductively into the sensitive sensor node.
4

Why is a datasheet reference layout especially important for RF or switching circuits?

Answer: Parasitics and current-loop geometry are part of the circuit behavior; small layout changes can materially alter performance.
5

Does placing analog and digital blocks far apart always improve performance?

Answer: No. Excessive distance can lengthen signals and returns. Place blocks to keep sensitive paths short while controlling noisy current flow.

10 — Advanced

10. Signal integrity and high-speed routing

Core knowledge

  • Signal edge rate—not only clock frequency—determines when a trace behaves as a transmission line.
  • Controlled impedance requires a defined stack-up and continuous reference plane.
  • Route differential pairs with the specified impedance geometry, low discontinuity and required skew control.
  • Use terminations, length matching and via transitions only when the interface or analysis requires them.

Practical milestone: Define stack-up-based constraints for one interface such as USB, Ethernet, LVDS or a fast clock.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

A 1 MHz signal has a 500 ps edge. Can it require high-speed treatment?

Answer: Yes. The fast edge contains high-frequency energy; interconnect delay relative to edge time is what matters.
2

Can you set a 50 Ω trace width before knowing the stack-up?

Answer: No. Width alone is insufficient; dielectric height, material properties and copper thickness are required.
3

Why avoid changing differential-pair spacing around vias?

Answer: Spacing changes alter coupling and differential impedance, creating a discontinuity.
4

Must every pair be length-matched to zero difference?

Answer: No. Match only to the interface’s permitted skew; unnecessary serpentine adds loss and coupling.
5

What is a stub?

Answer: A branch of transmission line that ends away from the main signal path; at high speed it can create reflections and resonances.

11 — Advanced

11. EMC, EMI and ESD-aware layout

Core knowledge

  • Reduce loop area, especially for fast switching and high di/dt paths.
  • Keep noisy nodes compact, separate them from sensitive traces and use filtering at cable/connector boundaries.
  • Give ESD currents a short, controlled path that avoids protected circuitry.
  • Plan pre-compliance measurements; EMC fixes after enclosure tooling and production are expensive.

Practical milestone: Review a completed PCB for likely radiation, susceptibility and ESD-current paths before ordering.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

What PCB property commonly increases magnetic-field radiation?

Answer: Large current-loop area, particularly with fast-changing current.
2

Why should a switch node copper area be limited?

Answer: It has fast voltage transitions and can capacitively couple noise and radiate.
3

Is a TVS diode enough regardless of placement?

Answer: No. Long traces and inductive return paths allow the transient voltage to reach the protected circuit.
4

Why filter signals near a cable connector?

Answer: The cable can act as an antenna or noise entry path; filtering at the boundary limits energy entering or leaving the board.
5

What is pre-compliance testing?

Answer: Early, lower-cost emissions/immunity testing used to find likely failures before formal certification.

12 — Production readiness

12. DFM/DFA, fabrication files, assembly and board bring-up

Core knowledge

  • DRC checks your encoded rules; DFM/DFA checks whether the chosen fabrication and assembly process can build the design reliably.
  • Release the required manufacturing data: copper/mask/silkscreen/outline plots, drill data, BOM, pick-and-place and drawings/notes as applicable.
  • Inspect every output in an independent viewer before ordering.
  • Bring up the first board methodically: visual inspection, shorts check, current-limited power, rails, clocks, reset, programming and interfaces.

Practical milestone: Order, assemble and document one revision of your own board, including measured faults and design corrections.

Illustration examples

Exercises

1

Does a clean DRC guarantee manufacturability?

Answer: No. DRC only checks the rules you configured; missing or incorrect process rules can still produce an unbuildable design.
2

Which files define drilled holes?

Answer: NC/Excellon drill files or the equivalent format required by the selected fabricator.
3

Why inspect Gerbers in a separate viewer?

Answer: It checks the actual exported manufacturing data rather than assuming the CAD view and plot settings are correct.
4

What is the safest first-power method for a prototype?

Answer: After visual and resistance/continuity checks, use a current-limited bench supply and monitor current and rail voltages.
5

What should be checked before firmware features?

Answer: Power rails and sequencing, excessive current or heat, clocks, reset and boot configuration; firmware cannot compensate for missing hardware fundamentals.
