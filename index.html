1. PCB fundamentals, workflow, and KiCad setup

Milestone: Create a clean KiCad project and explain the complete PCB workflow.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Understand the objects

    A schematic describes electrical intent: components, pins, and connections. A PCB is the manufactured bare board. A PCBA is the board after components are assembled. A symbol is not a physical package; a footprint is not an electrical model.

  2. Choose a safe first scope

    Begin with a low-voltage, low-current, two-layer board such as a battery-powered LED board or a simple sensor breakout. Do not start with mains electricity, lithium-cell charging, high-current motor power, RF, DDR memory, or safety-critical hardware.

  3. Install and open KiCad

    Use the current stable KiCad release. Create a new project rather than opening isolated schematic or board files. A normal project contains .kicad_pro, .kicad_sch, and .kicad_pcb files.

  4. Learn the normal workflow

    Write requirements → draw a block diagram → capture the schematic → run ERC → assign and verify footprints → prepare the BOM → define stackup and design rules → place components → route → run DRC → inspect in 3D → generate fabrication and assembly files → manufacture → assemble → bring up and test.

  5. Control versions

    Keep the entire project folder together. Use clear revision names and Git when possible. Do not email only the board file; libraries, project rules, documentation, and manufacturing outputs may also be required.

  6. Make one tiny board end to end

    Complete the whole process once before attempting a larger design. A finished simple board teaches more than an unfinished complex board because it exposes fabrication, soldering, and debugging issues.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1Put these activities in a defensible order: routing, schematic, DRC, requirements, fabrication files, ERC, placement, footprint verification.
Answer: Requirements → schematic → ERC → footprint verification → placement → routing → DRC → fabrication files. ERC belongs before layout because it checks schematic connectivity. DRC belongs after and during layout because it checks physical rules. Footprint verification must happen before ordering; a perfect route to the wrong package is still a failed board.
Exercise 2What is the difference between a symbol and a footprint?
Answer: A symbol is the logical representation used in the schematic; it shows pins and electrical meaning. A footprint is the physical copper, holes, mask, paste, courtyard, and graphics used on the board. One generic resistor symbol can map to many footprints. The pin numbers must agree between the symbol, footprint, and physical component.
Exercise 3Choose the better first PCB: (A) a 230 V AC motor controller or (B) a 5 V LED and push-button board. Explain.
Answer: B. It is low voltage, has few components, can be checked with a multimeter, and has low consequence if a mistake occurs. A includes shock, fire, creepage, clearance, isolation, thermal, switching-noise, and regulatory risks. It is unsuitable as an unsupervised first design.
Exercise 4A classmate sends only a .kicad_pcb file. What may be missing?
Answer: The schematic, project rules, net classes, design rationale, BOM, component attributes, project-specific symbols and footprints, simulation files, and revision history. The board may open, but the design cannot be fully reviewed or safely modified.
Exercise 5Does a clean ERC and DRC prove the PCB will work?
Answer: No. They prove only that configured rules were not violated. They do not verify component values, functional requirements, analog stability, firmware, thermal limits, connector pinouts, power sequencing, signal integrity, or whether the rules themselves were correct.

2. Schematic capture and electrical correctness

Milestone: Draw a readable schematic, calculate basic values, produce a BOM, and obtain a justified clean ERC.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Start with requirements and blocks

    State supply voltage, current, inputs, outputs, interfaces, environmental limits, and test needs. Draw functional blocks before placing individual symbols. This prevents a schematic from becoming an unstructured collection of parts.

  2. Select symbols by function and exact part

    Use generic symbols only while the exact part is undecided. Once selected, record the manufacturer part number, package, voltage rating, tolerance, and other critical attributes.

  3. Wire deliberately

    Use short local wires and named nets for longer connections. Give important signals meaningful names. A label creates electrical connectivity; two visually separate wires with the same valid label are the same net.

  4. Treat power as a circuit

    Show input protection, regulator, bulk capacitance, local decoupling, ground, and power-good or enable pins. Do not assume power pins are automatically handled. Read the device datasheet for every supply pin, exposed pad, reference pin, and required capacitor.

  5. Annotate and verify pins

    Every symbol needs a unique reference designator. Verify connector pin numbers, IC power pins, polarity, transistor pin order, diode orientation, and unused-pin requirements against datasheets.

  6. Run ERC critically

    Fix genuine errors. For intentional exceptions, document the reason instead of blindly excluding messages. ERC cannot detect a wrong resistor value or reversed connector if the schematic is electrically legal.

  7. Generate a useful BOM

    Include reference, quantity, value, manufacturer, MPN, package, tolerance/rating, approved substitute, and do-not-populate status where applicable.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1A 5 V supply drives a red LED with 2.0 V forward voltage at a target of 10 mA. Choose a resistor and check its power.
Answer: R = (5 − 2) / 0.01 = 300 Ω. Choose the next common conservative value, 330 Ω. Current ≈ 3/330 = 9.1 mA. Resistor power ≈ I²R = 0.0091² × 330 ≈ 0.027 W, so a 0.125 W or 0.25 W resistor has comfortable margin. Recheck using the LED datasheet's voltage range.
Exercise 2Two separated wires both have the label SENSOR_OUT. Are they connected?
Answer: In the same label scope, yes. Net labels create electrical connectivity without a drawn wire between them. The risk is accidental reuse or a spelling variation. Use descriptive names and inspect the highlighted net before layout.
Exercise 3ERC reports “power input not driven” on a battery-powered net. What should you do?
Answer: Confirm the battery symbol pin type and the actual source path. In KiCad, a PWR_FLAG may be needed to state that the net is driven. Add it only after confirming the physical source; do not use flags to conceal a genuinely unpowered rail.
Exercise 4A 4-pin connector is numbered left-to-right in your symbol, but the datasheet drawing is a bottom view. What is the risk?
Answer: The footprint can be mirrored relative to the cable or mating connector. Verify the manufacturer's viewing direction, pin-1 mark, mating-side orientation, and footprint pad numbers. Print the footprint at 1:1 and place the real connector on it before ordering.
Exercise 5List five schematic checks that ERC may not catch.
Answer: Wrong component value; inadequate voltage/current/power rating; reversed but electrically legal connector mapping; missing decoupling recommended by the datasheet; incorrect reset/boot strap; unstable regulator compensation; wrong analog reference; and incompatible logic levels. Any five with a clear explanation are acceptable.

3. Datasheets, components, packages, footprints, and libraries

Milestone: Select exact components and independently verify or create simple footprints.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Select an exact manufacturer part number

    A value such as “10 kΩ” is not enough. Check resistance/capacitance, tolerance, voltage, power, temperature coefficient, package, lifecycle, availability, and environmental requirements.

  2. Read datasheets in the right order

    Begin with features, recommended operating conditions, pin description, typical application, power requirements, layout guidance, package drawing, and ordering information. Absolute maximum ratings are survival limits, not normal design targets.

  3. Separate package from footprint

    The package is the physical component body and leads. The footprint is the PCB land pattern. Similar package names can have different pitches, body sizes, exposed pads, or pin numbering.

  4. Verify the pin map three ways

    Compare symbol pin number → footprint pad number → datasheet package pin. Confirm pin 1 visually. This is one of the highest-value manual reviews in PCB design.

  5. Inspect land-pattern layers

    Check copper pads, plated or non-plated holes, solder-mask openings, paste apertures, silkscreen, fabrication outline, courtyard, assembly origin, and 3D model orientation.

  6. Prefer project-controlled libraries

    Do not edit official libraries in place. Copy or create critical custom symbols and footprints in a project or company library under version control. Record the datasheet revision used.

  7. Perform a physical check

    Print at 1:1 or compare with a dimensional drawing. For connectors, switches, large capacitors, and unusual packages, place the real component on the printout or use a mechanical model.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1A device is available in SOIC-8 and TSSOP-8. Can you use either 8-pin footprint?
Answer: No. Equal pin count does not imply equal pitch or body dimensions. Compare pitch, lead width, body width, package length, pin-1 convention, and exposed-pad options. Select the exact land pattern recommended for the ordered package code.
Exercise 2Your symbol pin 1 is VCC, but footprint pad 1 reaches the IC’s reset pin. What happens?
Answer: The manufactured board connects VCC to reset and all other mapped pins may also be shifted. ERC and DRC can still pass because both trust the assigned numbering. Correct the symbol or footprint mapping before layout and re-run the three-way pin audit.
Exercise 3Choose between 0402 and 0805 passives for a first hand-assembled board.
Answer: Prefer 0805 or 0603 unless size or electrical performance requires 0402. Larger parts are easier to identify, place, solder, probe, and rework. Check voltage and power ratings; package size also affects capacitor effective capacitance and resistor dissipation.
Exercise 4Give a footprint-review procedure for a new connector.
Answer: Verify exact MPN and mating part; identify drawing view; confirm pin 1; copy pad coordinates and hole tolerances; check plated versus non-plated holes; add mechanical pegs; set courtyard and board-edge relationship; inspect in 3D; print 1:1; compare with the real connector; then have another person review the pinout.
Exercise 5Which BOM fields are essential for a production-ready resistor line?
Answer: References, quantity, resistance, tolerance, power, voltage rating when relevant, package, technology or temperature coefficient when critical, manufacturer, MPN, approved alternate, lifecycle status, and DNP status. Distributor stock codes alone are insufficient because they can change.

4. Stackup, fabrication limits, and design rules

Milestone: Configure a manufacturable two- or four-layer board using supplier rules and justified net classes.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Ask the manufacturer first

    Obtain supported layer counts, finished thickness, copper weight, minimum trace/space, drill sizes, annular ring, board-edge clearance, solder-mask limits, impedance options, and panel requirements. Do not design to arbitrary internet values.

  2. Choose the stackup for the circuit

    A two-layer board is economical for simple low-speed circuits. A four-layer board provides a continuous reference plane, easier power distribution, lower loop inductance, and more predictable signal integrity. More layers do not fix poor placement.

  3. Set absolute constraints

    Configure the smallest allowed clearance, track width, via, drill, annular ring, copper-to-edge, and silkscreen dimensions from the chosen fabrication capability. Using the supplier’s minimum everywhere may increase risk and cost.

  4. Create net classes

    Use different rules for ordinary signals, power, high current, sensitive analog, differential pairs, and controlled-impedance nets. A net class should express a requirement, not simply a color preference.

  5. Size conductors thermally

    Trace current capacity depends on copper thickness, width, allowable temperature rise, adjacent copper, board construction, and airflow. Use IPC-2152-based tools or manufacturer guidance rather than one unqualified formula.

  6. Plan vias and holes

    Confirm finished drill size, plating, annular ring, and aspect ratio. Use larger beginner-friendly vias unless density requires smaller features. Distinguish plated through holes from mechanical NPTH holes.

  7. Lock rules before detailed routing

    Rules can change, but late changes cause rework. Record the manufacturer and process assumptions in the project notes so a future order does not silently use incompatible capabilities.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1A fabricator advertises 0.10 mm minimum trace/space. Should every signal use 0.10 mm?
Answer: No. A published minimum is a process limit, often with lower yield or higher cost. Use wider traces and larger clearances where space permits. Reserve minimum features for necessary escape routing and confirm any price or reliability impact.
Exercise 2When is a four-layer board justified for a beginner project?
Answer: When the board has fast edge rates, dense routing, mixed analog/digital sections, USB or similar interfaces, difficult return paths, or meaningful EMI risk. A common useful arrangement is signal/components, solid ground, power, signal. Confirm the actual stackup with the fab.
Exercise 3Define three net classes for a 5 V sensor board.
Answer: Example: Signal for ordinary GPIO and low-current nets; Power for 5 V, 3.3 V, and pump/relay supply with wider copper and suitable vias; Analog for sensor inputs with routing/spacing rules that keep them away from switching nodes. Exact widths must be based on current, stackup, and manufacturer capability.
Exercise 4Why is “1 A trace width = X mm” not universally correct?
Answer: Temperature rise also depends on copper thickness, internal versus external layer, length, nearby planes, board material, airflow, ambient temperature, and duty cycle. Use an IPC-2152-based calculation or a fabricator-approved rule and document the assumptions.
Exercise 5A component lead is 0.8 mm maximum diameter. Is a 0.8 mm finished hole adequate?
Answer: Usually not. Assembly needs insertion clearance and fabrication has tolerances. Choose a larger finished hole based on the component drawing, lead tolerance, plating, and assembler guidance; then ensure sufficient annular ring and pad-to-copper clearance.

5. Component placement, power integrity, decoupling, and grounding

Milestone: Create a floorplan with short power loops, correct decoupling, and uninterrupted return paths.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Floorplan by function and current flow

    Place connectors and mechanical items first. Group power input, regulators, MCU, sensors, analog, drivers, and communication interfaces. Follow the signal and power path rather than filling empty space.

  2. Place critical parts before ordinary passives

    Place oscillators, switching-regulator loops, decoupling capacitors, analog references, termination resistors, and protection devices according to datasheet guidance. Then place less critical parts.

  3. Minimize the decoupling loop

    The capacitor, IC power pin, and ground return form a high-frequency current loop. Place the correct small ceramic capacitor close to the supply pin, with short and wide connections and a direct low- inductance ground path. Bulk capacitance serves slower load changes and belongs near the load or rail entry.

  4. Use a continuous ground reference

    For most beginner digital and mixed-signal boards, a solid ground plane is safer than splitting ground. Separate noisy and sensitive circuits primarily by placement and routing. Never route a fast signal over a plane gap without a deliberate return path.

  5. Control high-current and switching loops

    Identify the exact loop that carries pulsed current. Keep it compact, use sufficient copper, and keep it away from analog inputs, crystals, antennas, and connectors that can radiate noise.

  6. Place protection at the entry point

    ESD and surge components must intercept energy before it travels across the board. Place them beside the connector with a short path to the appropriate ground or chassis reference.

  7. Review thermal and assembly needs

    Leave copper and airflow for hot parts, keep heat-sensitive components away, respect courtyards, and provide probe access. A board that routes easily but cannot be soldered or tested is not a good layout.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1An MCU has four VDD pins. Is one 0.1 µF capacitor on the opposite side of the board enough?
Answer: Usually no. Follow the MCU datasheet; commonly each supply pin or supply group needs local high-frequency decoupling, plus bulk capacitance for the rail. Place capacitors close, minimize loop inductance, and use short ground vias. Do not invent values when the datasheet specifies them.
Exercise 2Place these blocks: power connector, regulator, MCU, analog sensor input, motor driver, external connectors.
Answer: Put external connectors at board edges. Place input protection and regulator near the power connector. Keep the motor driver and its high-current loop close to the motor/power connectors. Place the MCU centrally for routing. Put the analog front end near the sensor connector and away from the motor and switching nodes. Preserve a continuous ground reference.
Exercise 3Should analog ground and digital ground always be split?
Answer: No. A split can force return current to take a long path and create a larger radiating loop. For many mixed-signal boards, use one solid plane and separate noisy/sensitive areas by placement. Follow the converter or IC datasheet when it gives a specific grounding architecture.
Exercise 4Where should a USB ESD array be placed?
Answer: Immediately beside the USB connector, before the protected traces travel across the board. Keep the path from connector to protector and from protector to the chosen ground reference short and low inductance. Avoid stubs and preserve differential-pair geometry.
Exercise 5A switching regulator is stable on a breadboard but noisy on the PCB. What layout areas should be checked first?
Answer: Compare the layout with the regulator reference design. Inspect the hot switching loop, input capacitor placement, switch-node copper area, feedback routing, ground return, inductor/diode placement, and separation from sensitive signals. Component values alone cannot compensate for a poor power-stage layout.

6. Routing, vias, copper zones, and return paths

Milestone: Route a two-layer board with justified widths, minimal loop area, and zero unexplained DRC violations.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Route by priority

    Route fixed and critical nets first: high-current paths, clocks, crystals, analog inputs, feedback, differential pairs, and timing-sensitive interfaces. Route ordinary GPIO later.

  2. Think in complete current loops

    Every signal has an outgoing path and a return path. At higher frequencies the return tends to follow the signal’s reference plane. A route is incomplete as an engineering decision until its return is understood.

  3. Keep references continuous

    Avoid routing fast signals across plane splits, slots, board cutouts, or gaps in copper. If a signal changes reference plane, provide a nearby return path such as a ground stitching via where appropriate.

  4. Use vias intentionally

    A via adds inductance, consumes routing space, and changes the reference structure. It is not inherently bad, but unnecessary transitions complicate current return and high-speed behavior.

  5. Route power for current and transient demand

    Use widths and via counts based on current, temperature, and transient needs. Avoid narrow neck-downs in otherwise wide power paths. Place multiple vias where current must move between layers.

  6. Use copper zones correctly

    Zones are useful for ground and power, but they can form isolated islands, narrow slivers, or accidental antenna-like shapes. Refill zones and inspect actual connectivity after every significant placement change.

  7. Prefer clarity over myths

    Forty-five-degree corners are readable and easy to edit, but corner angle is rarely the dominant issue on ordinary low-speed boards. Continuous reference, short critical paths, controlled loop area, correct width, and adequate clearance matter more.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1Which should be routed first: LED GPIO, crystal traces, or an I²C line?
Answer: Crystal traces first because oscillator nodes are sensitive and usually have strict placement and routing guidance. I²C follows, especially if long or externally connected. The LED GPIO is normally least critical. Always defer to the component datasheet.
Exercise 2A fast signal crosses a slot in the ground plane. What is wrong?
Answer: Its return current cannot stay under the trace and must detour around the slot or through a distant connection. This enlarges loop area and can increase EMI, crosstalk, and signal distortion. Reroute the signal over continuous reference copper or redesign the reference transition.
Exercise 3A 1 A power route is 1 mm wide except for a 0.15 mm section between pads. Is the route effectively 1 mm wide?
Answer: No. The narrow section is the electrical and thermal bottleneck. Reposition parts, widen the neck, use a polygon, or add parallel copper/vias as justified. Verify the whole path, including pads, vias, connectors, and return current.
Exercise 4Why can a ground pour on a two-layer board still be poor?
Answer: Signal tracks and component pads can cut it into narrow paths or islands. A visually large pour may not provide a continuous low-impedance return. Inspect filled geometry, highlight the GND net, remove islands, add stitching vias, and reroute traces that create barriers.
Exercise 5Are 90° corners automatically defective?
Answer: No. On ordinary low-speed boards they are rarely the main risk. Use 45° or arcs for clarity and controlled geometry, but prioritize reference continuity, impedance requirements, spacing, loop area, and manufacturability. At very high speed, model the complete interconnect rather than applying a corner myth in isolation.

7. Signal integrity, differential pairs, EMI, and ESD

Milestone: Recognize high-speed risks, configure differential constraints, and review an interface using the device reference design.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Judge speed by edge rate

    A low clock frequency can still behave as a high-speed signal when rise and fall times are fast. When interconnect delay is no longer negligible compared with edge time, transmission-line behavior matters.

  2. Use the correct reference geometry

    Microstrip and stripline impedance depend on trace width, copper thickness, dielectric thickness, dielectric constant, and nearby reference planes. Ask the manufacturer for a controlled-impedance stackup.

  3. Route differential pairs as a system

    Maintain the specified differential impedance, spacing, reference plane, pair coupling, and allowable intra-pair skew. Keep the pair together through connectors and vias, and minimize uncoupled sections.

  4. Do not length-match without a requirement

    Length tuning is not a decorative step. Match only nets that have a timing or skew requirement, and use the allowed tolerance from the interface or component documentation. Excessive serpentine routing can add coupling and loss.

  5. Reduce crosstalk and emissions

    Increase spacing between unrelated fast signals, avoid long parallel runs, keep clocks away from board edges and sensitive analog nodes, and maintain compact current loops. Slower driver edges may help when the interface permits them.

  6. Design ESD paths, not just ESD parts

    A protection component works only with a short, low-inductance discharge path. Place it at the connector, avoid routing protected and unprotected traces together, and follow the protector’s layout example.

  7. Use reference designs carefully

    Copy critical placement and routing from the exact device evaluation board where possible. Confirm that your stackup, connector, package, and operating mode match; a reference layout is not universal.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1A 1 MHz GPIO has a 1 ns rise time and runs across a long board. Is it automatically low speed?
Answer: No. The 1 ns edge contains much higher-frequency energy than the 1 MHz repetition rate suggests. Compare interconnect delay with edge time and treat the route as a transmission line when delay is significant. Use the driver and receiver documentation or SI analysis for exact limits.
Exercise 2What information is required before routing a 90 Ω USB differential pair?
Answer: The manufacturer’s stackup, copper thickness, dielectric thickness and material, target differential impedance and tolerance, allowed width/gap, connector and IC pin geometry, via strategy, maximum skew or mismatch, ESD part layout, and any reference-design requirements.
Exercise 3One member of a pair is made longer with a large serpentine beside the other member. Is this always good?
Answer: No. It may reduce pin-to-pin length difference but create local crosstalk, loss, and uneven coupling. First improve placement and breakout. Tune only the necessary amount, use the EDA pair-skew tool, and respect minimum spacing within the serpentine.
Exercise 4Can a differential pair cross a split plane because its currents cancel?
Answer: Do not assume so. Real pairs have common-mode current, imbalance, connector discontinuities, and imperfect coupling. A continuous reference plane remains important. Follow the interface and device layout guidance, and avoid plane gaps under the pair.
Exercise 5An interface passes functional testing but fails radiated-emissions testing. List first layout checks.
Answer: Inspect clock and fast-edge loop areas, return-plane continuity, traces near board edges, cable/connector common-mode paths, decoupling loops, switching-regulator hot loops, shield or chassis connections, ESD placement, and long parallel routes. Passing functional tests does not demonstrate EMC compliance.

8. DFM/DFA, fabrication outputs, assembly, bring-up, and debugging

Milestone: Order, assemble, and safely bring up a documented board revision.

Theory: step-by-step beginner guide

  1. Review for manufacturing and assembly

    Check board outline, holes, slots, edge clearances, solder-mask slivers, paste apertures, courtyard overlap, component orientation, polarity marks, fiducials, tooling needs, panel constraints, and parts that cannot be assembled by the chosen process.

  2. Run final consistency checks

    Update PCB from schematic, refill zones, run ERC and DRC, inspect schematic–PCB parity, review every waiver, and compare footprints with libraries. Zero unexplained errors is the target.

  3. Inspect mechanically

    Use 3D view and exported STEP where useful. Check connector access, enclosure interference, mounting holes, board edge, component height, cable bend space, and tool access. A 3D model is helpful but not a substitute for dimensioned mechanical drawings.

  4. Generate the right package

    Typical bare-board files include Gerber or another accepted fabrication format, drill files, board outline, and fabrication notes. Assembly may also require BOM, centroid/position file, assembly drawing, paste layers, polarity information, and approved substitutes.

  5. Verify outputs independently

    Open the generated Gerbers and drills in a viewer, not only in the PCB editor. Confirm layer alignment, mask, silkscreen, drill positions, outline, and that the fabrication folder contains the intended revision.

  6. Plan safe bring-up

    Before power: visual inspection, continuity and resistance checks, polarity check, and power-rail-to-ground short checks. First power with a current-limited bench supply where appropriate. Verify input current and power rails before fitting or enabling expensive devices.

  7. Debug systematically and feed back

    Separate power, clock/reset, programming, communication, and functional tests. Record measurements, failures, bodge wires, rework, and root causes. Convert lessons into schematic, footprint, rule, checklist, and test-point improvements for the next revision.

Five exercises with detailed answers
Exercise 1List a minimum fabrication and assembly package for a two-layer SMT board.
Answer: Fabrication: top/bottom copper, top/bottom solder mask, required silkscreen, Edge.Cuts/outline, plated and non-plated drill data as required, stackup/material notes, finished thickness, copper weight, finish, and revision. Assembly: BOM with MPNs and DNPs, centroid/position file, assembly drawing, paste layers or stencil data, polarity/orientation notes, and approved alternates.
Exercise 2DRC reports copper too close to the board edge, but the manufacturer says it may be acceptable. What should you do?
Answer: Obtain the manufacturer’s written rule for that exact feature and process. Prefer correcting the design. If an exception is necessary, document it in the design review and fabrication notes, configure a specific rule or exclusion, and avoid globally disabling the check.
Exercise 3Describe a safe first-power sequence for a new 5 V board.
Answer: Inspect assembly and polarity; check resistance from 5 V and each rail to ground; verify no solder bridges; set a bench supply to 5 V with a conservative current limit; power while watching current; stop on unexpected current or heating; measure all rails and reset/enable states; then connect the programmer and test one subsystem at a time.
Exercise 4The 3.3 V rail is at 0.4 V and the supply current limit is active. What is the first debugging direction?
Answer: Treat it as a short or overload. Power off. Measure resistance to ground, inspect for solder bridges and reversed parts, divide the rail by removing jumpers/ferrites or components, check regulator pinout and footprint, and use controlled current injection/thermal methods only if trained. Do not repeatedly increase the current limit.
Exercise 5A board works only after a bodge wire is added. Is the project finished?
Answer: It may be usable as a prototype, but the design is not finished. Record the wire endpoints, electrical reason, root cause, affected files, test result, and required revision. Update the schematic first, synchronize the PCB, re-run reviews, and mark the assembled board with its actual hardware revision.
